📄 Course context & structural rules

This course is part of an AAS Business degree for career-switching adults. Students have completed Business Psychology (PSYC140) and Management Fundamentals in Term 1. Each lesson is a standalone, 15–40 minute AI-tutor session. Design constraints: every lesson connects to a specific lifecycle phase; core concepts appear in at least 3 lessons across different units; no lesson introduces more than 2 new concepts; team and stakeholder dynamics appear in execution and control units; each unit has 8–10 lessons.

🧭 Agent: Learning Arc Designer
i

What it is: The learning arc is the course's identity and learning flow — what changes from lesson to lesson and unit to unit, why it changes, and how the course systematically builds toward mastery. It turns the syllabus into a structured journey rather than a topic list.

Learning arc: The project lifecycle arc

Project management is a lifecycle discipline — practitioners define, plan, execute, and close projects in sequence. Organizing the course this way gives career-switching adults a reusable mental model they can apply on day one of any PM role.

  • Defining the project: Establishes what projects are, how to scope them, who the stakeholders are, and which methodology fits — giving students the foundation to make informed planning decisions.
  • Planning the work: Builds the core planning toolkit: WBS, scheduling, resource allocation, and budgeting — so students can produce a workable project plan.
  • Leading the execution: Shifts from planning to doing: managing teams, handling conflict, identifying and mitigating risks in real time, communicating with stakeholders, and tracking progress with PM tools.
  • Controlling and closing: Addresses performance measurement, quality evaluation, cost management, course-correction, and formal project closure with lessons learned.

Organizing principle: Students begin by defining and scoping projects with clear boundaries, then develop the ability to plan work in detail, then manage the human and operational complexity of execution, and finally control outcomes and close projects professionally. By Unit 4, they can run a small project end-to-end and explain their decisions at each phase.

📚 9 Core Concepts (3 threshold)
ConceptDescription / role in course
Scope (threshold)The defined boundaries of what a project will and will not deliver, expressed as specific deliverables and exclusions. Introduced in Unit 1, spirals through all four units.
Work breakdown structure (WBS) (threshold)A hierarchical decomposition of all project work into smaller, manageable, and assignable tasks. Central to Unit 2, reinforced through scheduling and budgeting.
Project constraints and tradeoffs (triple constraint) (threshold)The interdependent relationship between scope, time, and cost — changing one forces adjustments in the others. Introduced in Unit 1, applied across all units.
RiskAn uncertain event or condition that, if it occurs, has a positive or negative effect on project objectives. Three-pass spiral: identification, assessment, real-time monitoring.
StakeholdersAny individual or group that can affect or is affected by the project, each with distinct interests, influence, and communication needs. Appears in all four units.
Scheduling and dependenciesThe process of sequencing tasks, estimating durations, identifying dependencies, and producing a timeline. Core to Unit 2.
PM methodology (waterfall vs. agile)The overarching approach that determines how a project is structured, planned, and executed. Introduced in Unit 1, grounded in practice in Unit 3.
Monitoring and controlThe ongoing process of comparing actual performance against the plan, identifying variances, and taking corrective action. Central to Unit 4.
Team leadership and conflict resolutionThe ability to build, motivate, and guide a project team, and to recognize and resolve interpersonal conflicts. Central to Unit 3.

Unit 2: Planning the work (10 lessons)

Builds the core planning toolkit: WBS, scheduling, resource allocation, and budgeting — so students can produce a workable project plan.

#TitleMastery outcomes
1Breaking work into pieces: building a WBSCreate a two-level WBS from a scope statement; Verify that a WBS is complete and mutually exclusive
2Estimating how long things actually takeApply at least two estimation techniques to project tasks; Identify common estimation biases and how to counter them
3Putting tasks in order: dependencies and sequencingIdentify dependency types between project tasks; Create a task sequence that respects dependencies
4Building a schedule that worksBuild a simple project schedule with milestones and dependencies; Identify the critical path in a project schedule
5Who does what: assigning real people with real limitsCreate a resource assignment matrix for a project; Identify resource conflicts and propose solutions that account for competing availability
6Building a project budget from the ground upBuild a bottom-up budget from WBS tasks and resource assignments; Distinguish between direct costs, indirect costs, and contingency reserves
7Planning for what could go wrongRate risks by likelihood and impact using a risk matrix; Create a mitigation plan for the top risks in a project
8Setting baselines you can measure againstIdentify the scope, schedule, and cost baselines for a project plan; Understand why baselines are necessary for measuring project performance
9Choosing the right tools for your projectSelect appropriate PM tools for different project needs; Set up a basic tracking board or Gantt chart for a project
10Putting the whole plan togetherAssemble a complete project plan from scope, WBS, schedule, budget, and risk components; Identify gaps or inconsistencies in a project plan
Unit 3: Leading the execution (9 lessons)

Shifts from planning to doing: managing teams, handling conflict, identifying and mitigating risks in real time, communicating with stakeholders, and tracking progress with PM tools.

#TitleMastery outcomes
1Kicking off the project with your teamCreate a kickoff meeting agenda that aligns the team on scope, roles, and timeline; Identify the elements that make a kickoff effective
2Building a team that actually works togetherIdentify the stages of team development and what each requires from a PM; Apply at least two motivation techniques to a project team scenario
3When team members disagree: managing conflictIdentify the type and source of a team conflict; Select and apply an appropriate conflict resolution approach for a given scenario
4Keeping stakeholders in the loop during executionCreate a status update tailored to different stakeholder groups; Identify when stakeholder expectations need to be actively managed
5Managing risks as they happenUpdate a risk register based on new information during execution; Decide when to escalate a risk versus handle it at the team level
6Tracking progress without micromanagingIdentify the key metrics for tracking project progress; Distinguish healthy progress tracking from micromanagement
7Dealing with scope creep in the middle of the projectIdentify scope creep in an active project scenario; Apply a change control process to evaluate a scope change request
8Making tradeoff decisions under pressureEvaluate a tradeoff decision using the triple constraint framework; Recommend a course of action when constraints conflict during execution
9Running agile in practice: the sprint cycleDescribe how a sprint cycle organizes work into a repeatable rhythm of planning, executing, and reviewing; Identify where standups and retrospectives fit within a sprint and what each contributes
Unit 4: Controlling and closing (11 lessons)

Addresses performance measurement, quality evaluation, cost management, course-correction, and formal project closure with lessons learned.

#TitleMastery outcomes
1Tracking progress with simple metrics and dashboardsTrack project progress using percent-complete and milestone-based metrics; Read a project dashboard to identify tasks that are on track, behind, or at risk
2Measuring project performance with earned valueCalculate planned value (PV), earned value (EV), and actual cost (AC) for a project scenario; Identify whether a project is ahead or behind schedule and over or under budget using EV data
3Reading the warning signs: variance analysisInterpret schedule variance (SV) and cost variance (CV) from provided earned value data; Explain what positive, negative, and zero variance values mean for a project's health
4Getting back on track: corrective actionIdentify root causes of project variances from performance data; Recommend specific corrective actions for common project variances; Distinguish corrective actions from preventive actions
5Keeping costs under controlIdentify the causes of budget overruns in a project scenario; Apply cost control techniques to bring a project back within budget
6Is the work good enough? Evaluating qualityCreate acceptance criteria for project deliverables; Identify quality issues before a deliverable reaches the stakeholder
7Handling change requests without losing controlEvaluate a change request using impact analysis across scope, schedule, and cost; Apply a formal change control process to approve or reject a change
8Negotiating with stakeholders when things go wrongPropose a resolution when stakeholders want conflicting things; Negotiate a project adjustment that satisfies key stakeholder priorities
9Closing the project: deliverables, sign-offs, and handoffsCreate a project closure checklist covering deliverables, sign-offs, and documentation; Identify what must happen before a project can be formally closed
10What went right, what went wrong: lessons learnedConduct a lessons-learned review for a completed project; Identify actionable improvements for future projects from retrospective data
11Running a project from start to finishApply the full project lifecycle to a realistic scenario from definition through closure; Identify which phase a struggling project needs to revisit and why
